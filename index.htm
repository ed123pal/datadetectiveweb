THE GOLDEN RULE: Be Curious

 

Introduction

This document shows excerpts from The Data Detective. Ive picked out lines that I believe highlight key points in each of the authors rules. In most cases, my excerpts are directly from the book, but in some places, Ive paraphrased.

 

I think the book is worth reading, but these notes will help me to review and possibly remember what I felt were important concepts. Heres a link to a book review that I think fairly describes the book.

 

I like this book for many reasons, including:

●        It reminds that data analysis is more than p-values and pandas

●        It cites interesting contemporary and historical examples

●        It is easy to read

 

Rule 1: Search Your Feelings

 

Page 24

 

When it comes to interpreting the world around us, we need to realize our feelings can trump our expertise.

 

We sometimes buy into statistical claims that even a moments thought would tell us cannot be true.

 

Human nature welcomes us to be fooled sometimes.

 

Page 25

 

If you have deep expertise on a topic, its easy for this knowledge to become a liability. You can be undone by an emotional response.

 

To be wiser about statistics, you need to be wiser about yourself.

 

All the statistical expertise in the world will not prevent your believing claims you shouldnt believe and dismissing facts you shouldnt dismiss.

 

You must complement your statistical expertise with control of your emotional reactions to the statistical claims you see.

 

Sometimes you wont have an emotional response to a statistical claim. Its more difficult when emotional responses are involved.

 

Before you use or repeat a statistical claim ask how it makes you feel. Check if your emotions are clouding your judgment.

 

Page 30

 

Motivated reasoning is thinking through a topic with the aim, conscious or unconscious, of reaching a particular kind of conclusion. We are more likely to notice what we want to notice.

 

Page 31

 

An art critic who loves Vermeer is motivated to conclude that painting in front of him is not a forgery, but a masterpiece.

 

Giving people more information seems to actively polarize them on the question of climate change. People strain to reach the conclusion that fits with their other beliefs and values. The more they know, the more ammunition they have to reach the conclusion they hope to reach.

 

Page 38

 

People tend to dismiss studies that contradict their cherished beliefs.

Worse, the more detail people are given they are more likely to use the details as a way to find holes in arguments that contradict their cherished beliefs.

 

If you give a person balanced pros and cons arguments for both sides of a controversial subject, they will latch onto the arguments that support their preexisting opinion and dismiss facts that support the side they oppose. This tendency is called biased assimilation of new information. In short, the more we know, the more biased were able to be.

 

People will go to great lengths to reject ideas that are uncomfortable and unwelcome.

 

Page 39

 

The social consequences of your beliefs are real and immediate. Historically, people have learned its safer to fit in with the people around you.

 

Short-term (at least) the consequences of being wrong on a topic like climate change are small, but being on the wrong side with your social group can have immediate and severe consequences.

 

Most everyone falls prey to motivated reasoning at times. We all think with our hearts sometimes instead of our heads.

 

Page 42

 

Our emotions can, and often do, shape our beliefs more than any logic. We can learn to control our emotions - that is part of the process of growing up. The first simple step is to notice those emotions. If you see a statistical claim that upsets you, pay attention to your reaction.

 

If you can learn to notice and check your emotional reactions, the closer to the truth you can get. Acquire the habit stopping for a minute to think about a statistical claim.

 

It doesnt take a lot of intelligence and mathematical expertise to perform whats called cognitive reflection. Pause for a moment to check your cognitive reflection. Social media influencers want to excite you, but you need to resist.

 

People who want to make you give in to your emotional reaction will try to distract you with interesting details and sell you a story that you want to believe.

 

Page 47

 

Wishful thinking can turn a dubious statistic into solid evidence, and solid evidence into fake news. But, it doesnt have to if you have the discipline to stop and think.

Page 48

 

Evil people (I suggest like Trump) understand all too well that how we feel shapes what we think.

 

It is possible to take a stand not as a member of a political tribe but as someone who is willing to reflect and reason in a fair-minded manner.

 

Learn to master your emotions first and then as you develop the technical skills you will become a fully-capable data analyst.

Rule 2: Ponder Your Personal Experience

 

Page 49

 

Sometimes your personal experience contradicts credible-sounding statistics.

 

Examples: London bus and tube occupancy statistics vs. the crowds and wait times being experienced by the author.

 

Why such a difference between statistics showing there was not such a problem vs. what it felt like to use overcrowded mass transit.

 

Rule 1 says to not be fooled by your personal feelings. Who should you believe, a trusty spreadsheet or your lying eyes?

 

We need to be wise to determine when to trust statistics and when you trust your personal experience.

 

How to do the above:

●     Start by being curious about where the statistics come from, including how the counting is done and if there are places where assumptions (possible faulty ones) are being made.

●     Look for reasons why your personal experience might be so different. For example, think that in fact, people travel on crowded trains.

 

Page 52-53

 

London tube example:

Average occupancy could be a misleading statistic.

Ask what is the best way to measure customer experience?

Out of a hundred passenger journeys, how many are on crowded trains?

 

Consider that perhaps measurements should not focus on trains as much as focusing on passenger experience.

 

Sometimes both statistics and personal experience tell different stories and they are both true.

 

Page 55

 

When personal experience and statistics seem to be in conflict, a closer look may reveal why personal experience could be misleading.

 

Page 56

Famous people, such as celebrities, are not a random sample of the population. Yet, often we mistakenly think they are. Psychologists call this naive realism, which is the sense that we are seeing reality as it truly is, without filters or errors.

 

Naive realism is a powerful illusion. Its easy for your personal perspective to be badly out of step with credible statistics.

 

Page 58

 

Our perceptions of the world are often mistaken. One reason is because we often get our impressions from the media. News focuses on sensational and typically uncommon stories. These stories do not reflect everyday life. You can easily mistakenly get the sense that these stories are taking place in our living room.

 

News reports are data, but they are not representative data. But, these reports influence our view of the world.

 

Page 59

 

News gives quick-and-dirty fast statistics. They are immediate, intuitive, visceral and powerful.

You need to find ways to obtain slow statistics, which are based on a thoughtful gathering of unbiased information.

 

Page 60

 

Management or politicians can generate statistics and then incentives can make people take actions that appear to align with the statistics.

 

Page 61

 

Statistical metrics can be pernicious when they are being used to control the world, rather than to understand it.

 

Any observed statistical regularity will tend to collapse once pressure is placed upon it for control purposes. When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. The more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to control.

 

Page 65

 

Numbers will never tell the full story of what life on Earth is all about. But the stories that numbers tell do matter.

 

Worms-eyes and birds-eye views are both valuable. The trick is to determine how to get and balance the best of both. Both perspectives are needed to reinforce and correct each other. If the perspectives are in conflict, thats ok because that could motivate you to start investigating, i.e. ask questions.

 

Its easy to think that what you see with your eyes is the whole truth. It isnt. Understanding causation is tough even with good statistics, but hopeless without them.

Rule 3: Avoid Premature Enumeration

 

You can be great at data wrangling, correlating, regressing, etc. but its easy to make analysis blunders if you dont understand the origin and meaning of the numbers youre working with.

 

Page 70

The whole discipline of statistics is based on measuring or counting things.

 

Problem:

Measuring and counting is often not as straightforward as you might think.

 

Example 1

How many sheep are in the field?

Do you count lambs?

What if five sheep are expected to give birth next week?

 

Example 2

How do you count COVID-19 deaths?

If a person dies possibly from other causes but he had COVID-19 then do you also say he died of COVID-19?

Should you count people who died at home?

 

Statisticians often labor hard and end up producing bad information because they took a wrong turn at the outset. Too often they either count incorrectly or more often they rely on data gathered by others who also failed to count correctly. Or, statisticians not transparent about their counting assumptions or methodology.

 

Page 71

 

When you hear a claim that asserts a conclusion from a study its often written in a sensationalized way to attract as many ears or eyes as possible.

 

Problem:

The news item is often no more than a snippet, providing little if any context or details of how the study was conducted. This leaves you with no basis for evaluating the accuracy of the claim.

 

Example 1

Study claims that children who play violent video games exhibit violence in real life.

 

Questions:

Does play mean 20 minutes a day or 2 hours a day?

How were the children monitored?

What constitutes violent behavior, both in a game and in child behavior?

Which video games are violent?

Pac-Man goes around swallowing sentient beings alive.

Space invaders spend all their time shooting or avoiding being shot.

 

Page 78

First, clarify what is being measured and how. Next, break out the mathematics.

 

If you are wowed by some conclusion from a scientific and statistical study then hold on to your emotions and stop to think clearly to understand the data the claim is based on. Has the claim been vetted? Has the study been peer reviewed?

Rule 4: Step back and enjoy the view

 

Page 90

 

Step back and take a broader perspective when you hear a claim that sounds alarming, dramatic or surprising.

 

A headline can be narrowly correct but doesnt reflect the meaningful truth once you define the terms, evaluate any comparisons being made and understand the contexts directly and indirectly associated with the claim being made.

 

Example 1

 

Comparing murder rates for New York and London.

 

Page 91

 

Media outlets decide what to report as news depending on how frequently its readers check the outlet.

 

News outlets have a financial incentive to attract eyeballs as frequently and as long as possible. So theres a need for them to sometimes (perhaps often) construe trivial events as meaningful ones, often in a way that is sensationalized.

 

A weekly paper would report different things as news compared to a paper that published only once a year.

 

Example

Imagine a newspaper that publishes once every 50 years. Its 2018 edition headline would likely report the Cold War ended without the use of nuclear weapons. Other daily papers would likely publish some sensationalized comparatively trivial item.

 

We live in a world of fast-twitch media outlets. This means theres a good chance the outlets will often report items in a way that requires careful analysis to separate exaggeration or out of context claims from reliable and reasonable claims. Typically, readers/watchers are not able or willing to do so.

 

Page 92

 

A newspaper that publishes every two hundred years might have this news headline: Most people arent poor.

 

Page 95

 

Another way to step back and enjoy the view is to give yourself a sense of comparison. Faced with a statistic, ask yourself the question, Is that a big number? This is an unassuming but powerful question.

 

To understand a number, you need something to compare it with. We should carry a few landmark numbers in our head to allow for easy comparisons. Examples are: population of countries and the world, distance from Boston to Seattle, and GDP. Or, looking them up on Google is quick.

 

You cant rely on media outlets to give you context and perspective when they write headlines and accompanying articles. This is because these days people check the news frequently and so attention getting headlines is what gets people to keep checking. Also, people are drawn to bad news. Media companies want to attract and retain eyeballs because thats what drives their ad revenue. Its easier to make news seem bad if you omit context.

 

Page 98

 

In fact, its probably not that were drawn to surprising but not necessarily bad news. However, it does happen that the most surprising news is bad news. Good news tends to unfold slowly, but bad news tends to unfold quickly.

 

Media outlets routinely publish shocking news even if its not as significant as other news. In September 2001, 10 times as many Americans died from smoking than died in the terrorist attacks. Yet, there were no headlines about the smoking deaths that month.

 

Page 99

 

When media outlets want to grab our attention, they look for stories that are novel and unexpected over a short time horizon. These stories are likely to be bad news rather than good news.

 

Politicians know if they want to get in the headlines they need to make surprising claims. Same for others such as charities that need to raise money.

 

Page 103

 

Considering quitting the daily news. Weekly or better monthly news is less prone to sensationalism. Even better, a good book may provide better insights.

 

In short, slower-paced explanation and analysis is often better than breaking news.

 

Very little news requires immediate attention.

 

Even if you continue as a news junkie, also seek slower-paced, longer-term information.

They can provide statistical illumination.

Rule 5 Get the backstory

 

We often draw the wrong conclusions because we dont consider the entire picture.

 

Page 111

Cases of COVID-19 are subject to selective attention. People who feel terrible go to the hospital and get tested; people who feel fine dont. As a result, the disease looked even more dangerous than it really is. Statisticians understand the selective attention problem but it requires systematic testing to solve. Unfortunately, systematic testing was elusive in the early days of the pandemic when important policy decisions needed to be made.

 

Example 1

 

Page 112

WW2 US Air Force example

If we only look at the surviving planes (that returned after a WW2 bombing mission) we fall prey to survivorship bias. This means we completely misunderstand where the real vulnerabilities are. The holes in the returning planes do not signal the places that need to be reinforced.

 

Successes often receive attention while failures languish out of sight. This type of publication bias results in people only seeing a slice of the whole picture. Because of publication bias, people can form invalid conclusions about specific aspects of the world.

 

Example 2

People think that Kickstarter projects are smash hits. In reality, they arent and many requests dont even get funded. Successes are publicized but failures often get no notice.

 

Example 3

Researchers who try but fail to produce the same results from a study that produced a dramatic result often are unable to get a journal to publish their report about their investigation. The prior initial successful study got a bunch of publicity but ones that try and fail to get the same result dont get publicity. The initially published study that got the first word became, unfortunately, the final word.

 

Page 109

Even academic journals cannot be relied on to be free of publication bias. Even in these kinds of supposedly neutral journals there is a tendency for them to favor studies having positive or surprising results.

 

Page 111

We need to be aware that what we see around us is not always representative of the world; it is biased in systemic ways. As consumers of information, we sometimes consciously bring in our own ideological biases. However, many biases emerge from the way the world presents some stories to us while filtering out others.

 

Most books read are bestsellers, but most books dont become bestsellers and therefore are never heard about by most people.

 

Page 115

Publication bias is a form of survivorship bias. We never hear about the results of many studies because they dont survive, in the sense they are not published.

 

Page 116

Journals only want to publish studies that present eye-catching results. Researchers face the publish or perish problem so they are incentivized to push to get studies with surprising results published. Researchers resist digging too deep or having someone try to replicate their studies.

 

Page 117

Researchers know its unlikely they will get a study published if it refutes a previously published study with surprising results. The publish or perish dynamic means most researchers wont invest their time and other resources in studies that are unlikely to get published.

 

Perverse incentives abound that results in headlines that should be subject to skepticism unless you find out the backstory.

 

Page 118

Sometimes a researcher will find some promising results when doing a study but they are not statistically solid enough to publish. Some researchers will then incrementally collect and analyze more data to see if they can firm up the results. Studies based on large amounts of data are good but standard statistical tests on data collected bit by bit are not valid.

 

Page 119

Another reason the results from a study could be sensational but wrong is because researchers sometimes choose to measure the wrong thing or, even worse, initially measure multiple things and then later base the study results on the measured item(s) that produced the most interesting results, i.e. a form of cherry picking. Again, you need to understand the backstory.

 

Page 120

Another kind of publication bias is Hypothesizing After Results Known (HARKing). Preliminary data is often used to form a hypothesis. This is ok, but sometimes that preliminary data is used as all or part of the dataset to prove the hypothesis.

 

Page 121

What might seem like minor mistakes in collecting data combined with standard statistical methods can yield erroneous results. The standard statistical methods are designed to exclude most chance results. A combination of publication bias and loose research practices means there will sometimes be statistical accidents instead of valid discoveries.

 

Page 122

Accidental publication bias can be a bigger risk than weaponized publication bias.

 

Page 123

A study with striking results that has standard statistical process underpinning it might appear to signal that Disbelief is not an option., but in fact disbelief must always be an option. There is a reproducibility crisis.

 

Page 124

Interesting studies get published but too often their results are not valid as evidenced by failed efforts to replicate them.

 

Page 125

Publication bias (and survivorship bias) can have a big impact on our money and health.

 

Page 125

 

Money examples:

 

A third of the 43 companies that were the basis examples for the book In Search of Excellence were in financial trouble within two years after the book was published. Their apparent good fortune (perceived as excellence by Tom Peters) may have been random good luck. Its easy to be fooled by randomness. When good data is scarce, major decisions are too often made based on faulty research.

 

Page 126

 

We may think some investment managers are great based on a couple years of success, but we often never learn about failing funds they close down. Also, they rarely let us see a random sample of funds they have been offering.

 

The above is another example of survivorship bias (like the airplanes that survive to return after the mission). We often just look at the funds that exist today, i.e. we dont know about or consider the funds that no longer exist (i.e. the funds that have failed).

 

In short, you dont get to see the big picture.

 

Page 127

Survivorship bias even distorts some studies of investment performance.

 

If you ignore all the investment funds that quietly disappear, the apparent performance is twice as good as the actual performance.

 

People are convinced to pay high fees for actively managed funds but in fact for many of these funds their actual performance is no better than lower fee, passively managed funds.

 

Health examples

 

Even a randomized controlled trial (RCT) can be subject to publication bias. There can be unpublished studies that show a drug doesnt have the purported positive results.

 

Page 128

 

The problem of survivorship bias and the related form of it that is called publication bias still exists but is better understood now than in the past.

 

Page 129

 

Researchers need to be more transparent about data that was collected but not published. They also need to reveal the statistical tests that were performed and then set aside. The clinical trials that went missing in action. The studies that produced humdrum results that were not published.

 

The research community needs to see dead ends and the paths less traveled.

 

One (possibly correcting) approach was taken by a journal that would only publish studies that were pre registered. This can be a fix for publication bias. This can make more visible those studies that were pre registered but went missing in action. Researchers who try to verify the results can see if the original data analysis was followed, as opposed to the plan being changed once the data appeared. Unfortunately, journals can aspire to, but fail to enforce their standards.

 

Page 130

Social science research projects are difficult to pre-register because they often piggyback on government or charitable organizations projects. These projects also sometimes evolve over time in ways that researchers cannot predict or control.

 

Research standards are patchily enforced. Mostly its based on self-regulation.

 

There remain many reasons for these research problems still occurring, but there are signs of improvement. Some techniques and processes point to a potential for progress.

 

Page 131

 

Many statisticians say the concept of statistical significance is flawed. It does not embrace degrees of uncertainty. Conceptually, statistical significance is baffling, almost backwards.

 

Page 135

The Cochrane Collaboration is an international network of researchers who review, rate, synthesize, and publish the best available evidence on a huge variety of clinical topics. A related network, the Campbell Collaboration, aims to do the same thing for social policy questions.

Rule 6: Ask Who Is Missing

 

Studies too often fail to include a diverse set of participants. This problem relates to many characteristics such as age, gender, country, region, socioeconomic status and others.

 

Page 140

 

For example, all-male groups are less conformist than all-female groups.

 

Page 141

 

When looking at and making decisions based on the results of a study, its easy to incorrectly assume that data reflect an impartial view from nowhere when in fact they can contain deep and subtle biases.

 

Page 143

 

Even if (for example) women do get appropriately included in a survey, it sometimes happens that questions posed dont fit well for women since the questions were designed with men in mind.

 

It is all too tempting to assume that what we do not measure simply does not exist.

 

Page 144

 

An example of how important the above issue can be is a UK survey regarding sources of household income and who has power over the purse strings. In 1977 a child benefit was switched from a tax credit to a cash payment. The former typically put more money in the hands of men while the later put the money in the hands of women. When this happened then it was more likely the money would be spent on children.

 

Any dataset begins with somebody deciding to collect the numbers. What numbers are and arent collected, what is and isnt measured, and who is included or excluded are the result of all-too-human assumptions, preconceptions, and oversights.

 

For example, some countries dont collect good data about domestic violence. So, its difficult to track whether or not progress is being made.

 

Page 145

 

Surveys tend to be bundled with other projects. As a result, the things that are important to survey about or measure often dont get addressed.

 

Page 146

 

When it comes to the number of survey respondents, size can be valuable but its not the most important factor. A good example is the failure of the Literary Digest survey for the 1936 election. The failure was due to sampling bias (see below).

 

Page 147

 

Sampling error is when a randomly chosen sample doesnt reflect the underlying population purely by chance.

 

Sampling bias is when the sample isnt randomly chosen at all.

The Literary Digest mailed out surveys to people who had automobile registrations and phone directory listings. As a result, the survey went to disproportionately wealthier people. Also, Alf Landon supporters were more likely to mail back their survey.

 

Its important that a survey reaches a representative sample of the population.

 

Page 148

 

Dark data is missing data because people wont answer their phone or open their door. This is also called a non-response bias. Too many Trump supporters were missing from the surveys taken when polling was done ahead of the 2016 election.

 

Page 150-151

 

Big Data: N = All

But N = All is often more of a comforting assumption than a fact.

N = All is not the same as N = Everyone who has signed up for a particular service.

Netflix has copious data about Netflix customers, but far less data about people who are not Netflix customers. Netflix should not generalize from one group to the other.

 

Page 151

 

When data is scraped from cyberspace, they may be skewed in all sorts of awkward ways.

 

For example, you might think its better to run a sentiment analysis on Twitter rather than to commission a survey. In this case, youre going to learn what Twitter users think but that may not be representative of what the wider world thinks. In fact, Twitter users are not particularly representative of the wider world.

 

Page 152

 

N = All assumptions have led people astray.

Examples: misleading Twitter use stats during Hurricane Sandy

Example: iPhone app to detect potholes

 

Page 153

 

If algorithms are shown a skewed sample of the world, they will reach a skewed conclusion.

 

In general, what we count and what we fail to count is often the result of an unexamined choice, of subtle biases and hidden assumptions that we havent realized are leading us astray.

 

Unless we are collecting the data ourselves, we must ask who or what might be missing from the data were using.

 

In short, you must dig to see if the data youre using came from a study that had blind spots.

Rule 7: Demand Transparency When the Computer Says No

 

Page 156

 

Big Data can mean many things.

 

Heres one example: found data. Such as the digital exhaust of web searches, credit card payments, and mobile phone pinging the nearest cell tower.

 

An algorithm is a step-by-step recipe for performing a series of actions. In many ways, a computer program is an algorithm.

 

In recent years, the term algorithm has become known as a tool for finding patterns in large sets of data.

 

Google Flu Trends was built on pattern-recognizing algorithms churning through 50 million search terms.

 

Main topic of this chapter: the above kind of data and algorithms.

 

Page 156-157

 

Found datasets can be huge. There are many ways that data is collected continuously in real time in our current lives. The opportunities resulting from all these data are incalculable.

 

Big data cheerleaders make these claims:

 

#1 Data analysis produces accurate results.

 

#2 Every data point can be captured, N = All

 

#3 Scientific models are obsolete

 

Regarding #3, big data cheerleaders assert there is no need to develop and test theories about correlations. They say that with enough data, the numbers speak for themselves.

 

The Google Flu Trends failed in a few years and was shut down.

 

Page 158

 

Google did not know what linked the search term to the spread of flu. They were merely finding statistical patterns in data, which is what these algorithms do. There were some spurious correlations such as search terms correlated to high school basketball. Both flu and basketball get started in November.

 

The winter detector problem is common in big-data analysis.

 

Another example, pictures of healthy and cancerous skin usually had rulers in pictures of the latter. So the unhealthy algorithm became a ruler detector.

 

If we dont know why the algorithm is doing what its doing, were trusting our lives to a ruler detector.

 

Figuring out what causes what is hard - impossible some say.

 

Finding correlations is cheaper and easier than finding causation.

 

Theory-free analysis of mere correlations is inevitably fragile. If you have no idea what is behind a correlation, you have no idea what might cause that correlation to break down.

 

Google was never fully transparent about why its flu detector algorithm broke and why they didnt try to fix it.

 

Page 161

 

Purpose of this chapter:

Should we be more inclined to cheer the carpenters or worry about our unwitting role as nails?

 

Target and Tesco marketing anecdotes.

The algorithms are not working statistical miracles.

Whats happening under the magicians handkerchief is mundane.

 

Its easy to overestimate the capabilities of data-fueled computer analytics.

Theres a huge false positive issue.

Indeed, Target sends advertisements for baby wipes to a lot of women who are not pregnant. We dont hear about these events. The algorithms are not infallible.

 

Page 164

 

Tone down the hype:

Cambridge Analytica cannot read your mind.

Big Data cannot easily replace more plodding statistical processes such as the CDCs survey of influenza cases. The numbers do not always speak for themselves. Such claims are nonsense. Making big data work is harder than it seems.

 

Statisticians have spent 200 years figuring out what traps lie in wait when we try to understand the world through data. Even with big data and powerful computers, we must not pretend the traps have all been made safe. They have not.

 

Its a problem when people in power are overawed by algorithms they dont understand. Unfortunately, they then use them to make life-changing decisions.

 

For example, the IMPACT algorithm was used and then it resulted in the firing of effective teachers. It did not identify quality teachers and it could be fooled.

 

Blind credibility must not be given to the ability of algorithms to extract wisdom from data.

 

Garbage In, Garbage Out remains true no matter how much garbage you collect.

 

One problem is that algorithms can be trained using systematically biased datasets. Amazon tried using an algorithm to identify preferred applicants but it omitted qualified women because most past hires were men.

 

Page 169

 

Algorithms can be flawed.

Humans can be flawed.

 

Page 170

 

Human judges are inconsistent.

A researchers algorithm appeared capable of making better decisions about who to give bail to.

 

Judges (humans in general) have current offense bias.

 

Sometimes algorithms are better than humans and vice versa. It can be difficult to assess the performance of an algorithm.

 

Page 173

 

The problem is not entirely the algorithms. The problem is lack of scrutiny, transparency, and debate.

 

For example, alchemy vs science.

 

The mindset of scientists is that algorithms need to be analyzed, debated and improved on.

 

Page181

 

We shouldnt trust in general. Trust should be discriminating.

 

We should evaluate which algorithms we can trust and what we can trust them to do.

 

If we want to demonstrate that an algorithm is trustworthy, we need the basis of our decisions to be intelligently open.

Algorithms need to be:

●     Accessible

●     Understandable

●     Usable

●     Assessable

 

Page 182

 

Data scientists should form a professional society with a professional code of ethics.

 

Algorithms should be tested using randomized controlled trials. A real RCT may not be possible, but there could be similar solutions. Anyone who is confident of the effectiveness of their algorithm should be happy to demonstrate that effectiveness in a fair and rigorous test. Vital institutions such as schools and courts shouldnt use an algorithm on a large scale unless they are proved.

 

What sort of accountability or transparency is essential depends on what problem were trying to solve.

 

Page 184

 

To promote competition among algorithm developers, companies like Netflix and Amazon should give privatized access to their database to qualified researchers.

 

Page 185

 

Big data is often less trustworthy than small data. Small data can typically be scrutinized; big data tends to be locked away in vaults in Silicon Valley. Pattern-recognizing algorithms can all too easily by mysterious and commercially-sensitive black boxes.

 

Good judgment is needed.

 

Ask tough questions.

 

Do not simply trust algorithms.

 

If an algorithm is flawed, dont assume humans would be flawless.

Rule 8: Dont Take Statistical Bedrock for Granted

 

Page 187

 

The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) became respected as an authoritative source of information on the budget and economy.

 

Institutions like CBO are important and should be defended.

 

The CBO and OBR (UK) and similar statistical agencies need to assert their political independence.

 

Page 192

Rule 10: Keep an Open Mind

 

Page 246

Page 250

 

All of us are capable of filtering the information that comes our way, discarding some ideas and clinging on to others.

 

One of the reasons facts dont always change our minds is that we are keen to avoid uncomfortable truths.

 

On social media we can choose who to follow and who to block.

 

Worse. Even if you have to absorb unwelcome facts, you can selectively misremember them.

 

Page 251

 

Our emotions lead us to filter the most straightforward information.

 

Rather than to omit error and learn from it, people will change their recollections to ensure that no painful reckoning with reality is required.

 

Page 252

 

Experts can be terrible forecasters. Further, they can misremember their own forecasts.

 

Page 252

 

Traits of super forecasters.

 

Not subject-matter experts. Professors were no better than well-informed amateurs.

 

Not intelligence.

 

It did help if a person got some basic statistics training to turn their expertise into a sensible probabilistic forecast.

 

What helped the most was to encourage focus on base rates.

 

Example, at the wedding, you forecast if the marriage will last.

 

Instead of thinking about the many facts that are in front of us, zoom out and find one very straightforward statistic such as how many marriages end in divorce. This number is the base rate.

 

Page 256

 

Unless you have a base rate, the many other factors you may have available dont fit into any useful framework.

 

Ideally, a forecaster will combine the outside view (based on statistics) with the inside view (based on the specific case in front of him, e.g. the couple getting married.)

 

Its best to start with the outside view and then modify the forecast in light of personal experience. If you start with the inside view, you have no sense of scale and no frame of reference.

 

Third, super forecasters tend to update their forecasts frequently as new information emerges.

 

Fourth, super forecasters have an open-minded personality. They are actively open-minded thinkers. This means they dont cling too tight to a single approach. They are comfortable abandoning an old view in the light of fresh evidence or new arguments. They embrace disagreements with others as opportunities to learn.

The world is ruled by both numbers and feelings.

THE GOLDEN RULE: Be Curious

 

Its difficult to remember the 10 Rules of Thumb presented in this book each time you see a claim in traditional or social media. To the extent that you can, the rules can be useful in forming a preliminary assessment of the claim.

 

The 10 rules can be boiled down to one: Be Curious.

 

Look deeper and ask questions.

 

Page 269

 

In general, we should (we must) trust the use of numbers (statistics) to understand the world. But, we shouldnt trust without also asking questions.

 

Well-placed trust grows out of active inquiry rather than blind acceptance.

 

The questions we need to ask arent obscure or overly technical; they are what any thoughtful, curious person would be able to ask.

 

Different people reach different conclusions after seeing the same statistics due to tribalism. Toxic polarization. Curiosity is the antidote. Curiosity breaks the relentless pattern.

 

Page 271

 

Scientific curiosity is correlated to scientific literacy but they are different. Some curious people know little about science and some highly trained people have little curiosity.

 

The more curious we are the less our tribalism seems to matter.

 

There is little correlation between scientific curiosity and political affiliation.

 

People are good at filtering out or dismissing unwelcome information.

 

Page 272

 

Neuroscience studies suggest that the brain responds in much the same anxious way to facts that threaten our preconceptions as it does to wild animals that threaten our lives.

 

Its possible to incrementally cultivate curiosity in people.

 

Page 273

 

First reason: The level of curiosity in a population follows a bell curve. As such, perhaps its possible to nudge some people toward the right of the curve.

 

Second reason: Curiosity is often situational. We all have it in us to be curious, or not, about different things at different times.

 

Curiosity starts to glow when theres a gap between what we know and what we want to know. Curiosity is fueled once we know enough to know that we do not know. Alas, all too often we dont even think about what we dont know.

 

Page 274

 

The illusion of explanatory depth is a curiously killer and a trap. If we think we already understand, why go deeper? Why ask questions? Try to make people reflect on the gap in their knowledge.

 

Page 275

 

You can deflate somebodys overconfidence and moderate their politics simply by asking them to explain the details. Ask, what do you exactly mean by that. You may both find that you understand a little less, and agree a little more than you assumed.

 

Page 276

 

Orson Welles: once people are interested they can understand anything in the world. How to engage peoples interest is neither a new one or an intractable one.

 

People:

●     Love mysteries

●     Are drawn in by sympathetic characters

●     Enjoy the arc of a good story

●     Will stick around for a good story

 

Page 278

 

The communication model that uses humor (e.g. Stephen Colbert) can be effective in creating curiosity in people. If you want people to understand complex issues, you need to engage their curiosity. If people are curious, they will learn.

 

Page 279

 

This attitude is a problem: The people in this country have had enough of experts.

 

Experts are operating in a politically polarized environment. Any expert opinion will be fiercely contested by partisans.

 

Speaking slowly and clearly will only get experts so far. To communicate complex ideas, you need to spark peoples curiosity - even inspire a sense of wonder.

 

In short, engage their interest, i.e. awaken a sense of wonder.
